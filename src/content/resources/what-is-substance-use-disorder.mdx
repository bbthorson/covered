Substance use disorder (SUD) is a medical condition in which the use of one or more substances leads to clinically significant impairment or distress. It is recognized by the medical community as a chronic, treatable condition — not a moral failing or lack of willpower.

## How Common Is It?

According to SAMHSA's 2023 National Survey on Drug Use and Health:

- **48.7 million** Americans aged 12 or older had a substance use disorder in the past year
- Only about **24%** of people with SUD received any treatment
- SUD affects people of every age, race, gender, and socioeconomic background

## What Causes SUD?

Substance use disorder results from a combination of factors:

- **Genetics** — Family history accounts for roughly 40–60% of a person's vulnerability to addiction
- **Environment** — Stress, trauma, peer pressure, and early exposure to substances increase risk
- **Development** — Using substances during adolescence, when the brain is still developing, raises the likelihood of developing SUD
- **Mental health** — Conditions like depression, anxiety, and PTSD frequently co-occur with SUD

## How Is SUD Diagnosed?

The DSM-5 identifies 11 criteria for substance use disorder. A person must meet at least 2 within a 12-month period:

1. Taking the substance in larger amounts or for longer than intended
2. Wanting to cut down or stop but not being able to
3. Spending a lot of time getting, using, or recovering from the substance
4. Cravings or strong urges to use
5. Failing to fulfill obligations at work, school, or home
6. Continuing to use despite social or relationship problems
7. Giving up important activities because of use
8. Using in physically hazardous situations
9. Continuing despite knowing it causes physical or psychological problems
10. Needing more to get the same effect (tolerance)
11. Experiencing withdrawal symptoms when not using

The number of criteria met determines severity:

- **Mild**: 2–3 criteria
- **Moderate**: 4–5 criteria
- **Severe**: 6 or more criteria

## SUD Is Treatable

Like other chronic conditions such as diabetes or hypertension, SUD can be effectively managed with the right combination of treatments. Recovery is possible, and millions of Americans are living proof.

Treatment options include:

- **Behavioral therapies** (cognitive behavioral therapy, motivational interviewing)
- **Medications** (for alcohol, opioid, and nicotine use disorders)
- **Support groups** (mutual aid like AA, NA, SMART Recovery)
- **Residential or inpatient programs**
- **Outpatient programs** (varying levels of intensity)

The most effective treatment plans are individualized and often combine multiple approaches.
