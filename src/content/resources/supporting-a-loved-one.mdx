Watching someone you love struggle with substance use is painful. You may feel helpless, angry, scared, or guilty. These feelings are normal. While you can't force someone into recovery, you can take meaningful steps to support them — and yourself.

## Understanding What Your Loved One Is Going Through

Substance use disorder is a medical condition, not a choice. Your loved one is not using because they don't care about you. The disorder changes brain chemistry in ways that make it extremely difficult to stop, even when the person wants to.

Understanding this can help you:
- Respond with compassion instead of anger
- Set boundaries without guilt
- Recognize that relapse is a part of recovery, not a failure

## How to Help

### Start the Conversation
- Choose a time when they're sober and you're both calm
- Use "I" statements: "I'm worried about you" instead of "You need to stop"
- Be specific about behaviors you've noticed, not character judgments
- Listen more than you talk
- Don't expect one conversation to change everything

### Offer Concrete Support
- Research treatment options so you can share them when the person is ready
- Offer to help with logistics: making calls, transportation, childcare
- Attend a family therapy session if invited
- Learn about their specific substance and treatment options

### Set Healthy Boundaries
Boundaries are not ultimatums — they're limits you set to protect your own wellbeing:

- "I love you, but I can't give you money when I know it's being used for substances"
- "I won't cover for you at work anymore"
- "You're welcome to live here, but not if you're using in the house"
- "I'll support your recovery in any way I can, but I can't watch you use"

Follow through on boundaries consistently. Boundaries you don't enforce teach the other person they don't need to take you seriously.

### What NOT to Do
- **Don't enable** — Shielding someone from the consequences of their use delays their motivation to change
- **Don't shame or blame** — Guilt doesn't motivate recovery; it drives people further into substance use
- **Don't make threats you won't follow through on**
- **Don't try to control their behavior** — You can only control your own actions
- **Don't wait for "rock bottom"** — This is a myth. Treatment works at any stage

## Taking Care of Yourself

You cannot pour from an empty cup. Supporting a loved one with SUD is emotionally and physically exhausting.

### Support Resources for Families
- **Al-Anon** (al-anon.org) — For families and friends of people with alcohol problems
- **Nar-Anon** (nar-anon.org) — For families and friends of people with drug problems
- **CRAFT** (Community Reinforcement and Family Training) — Evidence-based approach that teaches families how to encourage treatment engagement
- **Family therapy** — Many treatment programs offer family sessions

### Self-Care Essentials
- Maintain your own health: sleep, nutrition, exercise
- Keep doing activities that bring you joy
- Talk to a therapist or counselor for yourself
- Connect with others who understand (support groups)
- Accept that you are not responsible for your loved one's choices
- Acknowledge your own feelings — grief, anger, and fear are all valid

## When There's a Crisis

If your loved one is in immediate danger:
- **Overdose**: Call 911 immediately. If available, administer naloxone (Narcan)
- **Suicidal thoughts**: Call 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline)
- **Immediate help**: Call SAMHSA's helpline at **1-800-662-4357**
