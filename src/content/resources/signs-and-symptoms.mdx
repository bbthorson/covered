Recognizing a substance use problem early can make a significant difference in outcomes. Here are the signs to look for — in yourself or someone you care about.

## Physical Signs

- Changes in appetite or sleep patterns
- Unexplained weight loss or gain
- Bloodshot eyes, dilated or constricted pupils
- Unusual smells on breath, body, or clothing
- Slurred speech or impaired coordination
- Frequent nosebleeds (may indicate snorted substances)
- Deterioration in physical appearance or personal hygiene
- Unexplained injuries or accidents

## Behavioral Signs

- Increased secrecy or lying about activities
- Sudden changes in friends, hobbies, or hangouts
- Unexplained need for money or financial problems
- Missing work, school, or important commitments
- Legal problems (DUI, possession charges)
- Engaging in risky behaviors while using
- Failed attempts to cut back or quit
- Using substances alone or in secret

## Emotional and Psychological Signs

- Mood swings or irritability
- Unexplained changes in personality or attitude
- Periods of unusual hyperactivity, agitation, or giddiness
- Lack of motivation or appearing "spaced out"
- Increased anxiety, paranoia, or fearfulness
- Sudden emotional outbursts

## When Does Use Become a Problem?

Not all substance use is a disorder. Use exists on a spectrum:

1. **Experimental use** — Trying a substance out of curiosity
2. **Social/recreational use** — Using in social settings
3. **Problematic use** — Use causing negative consequences
4. **Substance use disorder** — A diagnosable medical condition

The transition from recreational use to problematic use often happens gradually. Key indicators that use has become a problem include:

- **Loss of control** — Using more than intended
- **Continued use despite consequences** — Relationship, work, or health problems
- **Tolerance** — Needing more to achieve the same effect
- **Withdrawal** — Physical or psychological symptoms when not using
- **Centrality** — Substance use becoming the focus of daily life

## What to Do If You See These Signs

**If you recognize these signs in yourself:**

- [Take a confidential screening](/screen) to learn more about your situation
- Talk to a healthcare provider — they can help without judgment
- Call SAMHSA's helpline: **1-800-662-4357** (free, confidential, 24/7)

**If you recognize these signs in someone else:**

- Express concern without judgment — use "I" statements ("I've noticed..." "I'm worried...")
- Avoid ultimatums or confrontation when the person is under the influence
- Offer to help them find resources, but respect their autonomy
- Take care of your own wellbeing — supporting someone with SUD is challenging
- Consider Al-Anon or Nar-Anon for support for family members
