Medication-assisted treatment (MAT) combines FDA-approved medications with counseling and behavioral therapies. Research consistently shows MAT is one of the most effective approaches for treating opioid and alcohol use disorders.

## What Is MAT?

MAT is not "replacing one drug with another." These medications are carefully developed to:

- **Reduce cravings** so you can focus on recovery
- **Prevent withdrawal symptoms** that often lead to relapse
- **Block the euphoric effects** of substances, reducing the incentive to use
- **Normalize brain chemistry** disrupted by prolonged substance use

When combined with therapy and support services, MAT helps people sustain recovery and rebuild their lives.

## Medications for Opioid Use Disorder

### Buprenorphine (Suboxone, Sublocade)
- **How it works**: Partial opioid agonist — activates opioid receptors enough to reduce cravings and withdrawal, but not enough to produce a "high"
- **How it's taken**: Sublingual film/tablet (daily) or injection (monthly)
- **Key facts**: Can be prescribed by any licensed provider in an office setting; no special certification required since 2023
- **Duration**: As long as clinically indicated — there is no arbitrary time limit

### Methadone
- **How it works**: Full opioid agonist — satisfies opioid receptors, preventing withdrawal and cravings
- **How it's taken**: Liquid, tablet, or injectable; initially dispensed daily at a certified opioid treatment program (OTP)
- **Key facts**: Take-home doses become available as you stabilize; has been used effectively for over 50 years
- **Duration**: As long as clinically indicated

### Naltrexone (Vivitrol)
- **How it works**: Opioid antagonist — blocks opioid receptors completely, preventing any effect from opioids
- **How it's taken**: Monthly injection or daily oral tablet
- **Key facts**: Must be opioid-free for 7–14 days before starting; no abuse potential
- **Duration**: As long as clinically indicated

## Medications for Alcohol Use Disorder

### Naltrexone
- Blocks the pleasurable effects of alcohol
- Available as daily pill or monthly injection (Vivitrol)
- Reduces heavy drinking days and supports abstinence

### Acamprosate (Campral)
- Restores chemical balance in the brain disrupted by chronic alcohol use
- Reduces symptoms of protracted withdrawal (anxiety, insomnia, restlessness)
- Taken as a pill three times daily

### Disulfiram (Antabuse)
- Causes unpleasant physical reactions (nausea, flushing, headache) if alcohol is consumed
- Works as a deterrent — most effective for highly motivated individuals
- Taken as a daily pill

## Common Misconceptions

**"MAT is just substituting one addiction for another."**
MAT medications are prescribed at therapeutic doses that don't produce euphoria. They allow normal brain function and daily activities — just like medications for any other chronic condition.

**"You're not really in recovery if you take medication."**
The goal of recovery is improved health and quality of life. Medications that help achieve and sustain that goal are a valid and effective part of treatment.

**"MAT should only be short-term."**
Research shows that longer durations of MAT are associated with better outcomes. Discontinuation should be a collaborative decision between patient and provider, not driven by arbitrary timelines.

**"MAT is only for severe cases."**
MAT can be effective across the spectrum of severity. Early intervention with medication can prevent progression to more severe disorder.
