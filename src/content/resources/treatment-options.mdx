There is no single treatment that works for everyone. The most effective approach depends on the individual's specific needs, the substance(s) involved, and the severity of the disorder. Here is an overview of evidence-based treatment options.

## Levels of Care

Treatment is organized into levels based on intensity:

### Outpatient Treatment
- **Standard outpatient**: 1–2 sessions per week, allows you to maintain work and home life
- **Intensive outpatient (IOP)**: 9–20 hours per week, structured but flexible
- **Partial hospitalization (PHP)**: 20+ hours per week, medical monitoring during the day

### Residential / Inpatient Treatment
- **Short-term residential**: Typically 3–6 weeks of intensive treatment
- **Long-term residential**: 6–12 months, often includes therapeutic community model
- **Hospital-based inpatient**: For severe cases needing medical stabilization or detox

### Detoxification (Detox)
- Medically supervised withdrawal management
- Not treatment on its own — should be followed by ongoing care
- Can be inpatient or outpatient depending on the substance and severity

## Behavioral Therapies

Evidence-based therapies used in SUD treatment:

- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)** — Identifies and changes negative thought patterns and behaviors associated with substance use
- **Motivational Interviewing (MI)** — Helps resolve ambivalence about change and strengthens motivation for recovery
- **Contingency Management** — Provides tangible rewards for positive behaviors like maintaining sobriety
- **Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)** — Teaches coping skills for emotional regulation, useful for co-occurring conditions
- **12-Step Facilitation** — Structured approach to engaging with mutual aid groups like AA or NA

## Medications for Addiction Treatment (MAT)

FDA-approved medications are available for several substance use disorders:

### Alcohol Use Disorder
- **Naltrexone** — Blocks the rewarding effects of alcohol
- **Acamprosate** — Reduces withdrawal symptoms and cravings
- **Disulfiram** — Causes unpleasant effects when alcohol is consumed

### Opioid Use Disorder
- **Buprenorphine** (Suboxone) — Reduces cravings and withdrawal symptoms
- **Methadone** — Long-acting opioid agonist, dispensed through certified programs
- **Naltrexone** (Vivitrol) — Blocks opioid effects, available as monthly injection

### Nicotine Use Disorder
- **Nicotine replacement therapy** (patches, gum, lozenges)
- **Varenicline** (Chantix) — Reduces cravings and the rewarding effects of nicotine
- **Bupropion** (Wellbutrin/Zyban) — Reduces cravings and withdrawal symptoms

Medications are most effective when combined with behavioral therapies.

## Support Groups and Mutual Aid

- **Alcoholics Anonymous (AA)** / **Narcotics Anonymous (NA)** — 12-step fellowship programs
- **SMART Recovery** — Self-Management and Recovery Training, science-based
- **Celebrate Recovery** — Faith-based recovery program
- **Women for Sobriety** — Specifically designed for women
- **LifeRing** — Secular, abstinence-based peer support

## Choosing the Right Treatment

Consider these factors when selecting treatment:

1. **Substance(s) involved** — Different substances may need specific approaches
2. **Severity** — More severe disorders often need higher levels of care initially
3. **Co-occurring conditions** — Mental health conditions should be treated simultaneously
4. **Support system** — Residential care may be needed if the home environment isn't supportive
5. **Practical considerations** — Insurance coverage, location, work/family obligations
6. **Personal preferences** — Religious/spiritual orientation, gender-specific programs, language needs
