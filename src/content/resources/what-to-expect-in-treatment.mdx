Starting treatment can feel overwhelming. Knowing what to expect can help reduce anxiety and prepare you for the process ahead.

## Before Treatment Starts

### Insurance and Payment
- Most health insurance plans cover substance use treatment (required under the ACA)
- Medicaid and Medicare cover treatment services
- Many facilities offer sliding-scale fees based on income
- SAMHSA-funded programs may offer free or low-cost treatment
- Ask about payment options during your first call — don't let cost stop you from reaching out

### What to Bring (Residential Programs)
- Government-issued ID and insurance card
- List of current medications
- Comfortable clothing for several days
- Basic hygiene items (facilities often have restrictions on products containing alcohol)
- Important phone numbers written down
- Leave valuables at home

## The Intake Process

Your first appointment typically includes:

1. **Assessment** — A clinician will ask about your substance use history, medical history, mental health, family situation, and goals. Be honest — this information is confidential and helps create your treatment plan.

2. **Physical evaluation** — Basic health screening to identify any medical needs

3. **Treatment plan** — Based on the assessment, your care team will recommend a level of care and develop an individualized plan

4. **Orientation** — Overview of the program's rules, schedule, and what's expected of you

## During Treatment

### A Typical Day (Residential)
- Morning check-in or meditation
- Group therapy sessions (2–3 per day)
- Individual counseling (1–3 times per week)
- Educational workshops on addiction, coping skills, relapse prevention
- Meals and breaks
- 12-step or mutual aid meeting
- Free time, exercise, or journaling
- Evening reflection

### Common Therapy Modalities
- **Group therapy** — Sharing and learning with peers in similar situations
- **Individual therapy** — One-on-one work with a counselor on personal issues
- **Family therapy** — Repairing relationships and building a supportive home environment
- **Psychoeducation** — Understanding the science of addiction and recovery
- **Skills training** — Coping strategies, stress management, communication skills

## After Treatment (Continuing Care)

Treatment doesn't end when a program ends. Continuing care is essential for long-term recovery:

- **Step-down care** — Transitioning from a higher level (residential) to a lower level (outpatient)
- **Outpatient appointments** — Regular check-ins with a counselor
- **Support groups** — Ongoing participation in mutual aid programs
- **Sober living** — Structured housing for people transitioning out of residential treatment
- **Recovery coaching** — Peer support from someone with lived experience
- **Relapse prevention planning** — Identifying triggers and developing strategies

## Your Rights in Treatment

- You have the right to be treated with dignity and respect
- Your treatment information is protected by federal confidentiality laws (42 CFR Part 2)
- You can refuse any treatment or medication
- You can request a change in your treatment plan
- You can file a grievance if you feel your rights have been violated

## Tips for Getting the Most Out of Treatment

1. **Be honest** — with your care team and with yourself
2. **Stay open-minded** — treatments that seem unfamiliar can be effective
3. **Participate actively** — attend all sessions and engage in the process
4. **Build connections** — peer relationships formed in treatment can be lifelong supports
5. **Be patient** — recovery is a process, not an event
6. **Plan ahead** — work with your team on aftercare before you leave
